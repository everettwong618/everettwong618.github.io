MMET 281 · Spring 2026 · Individual project

Choosing how to make a door hinge and a pair of glasses.

Two parts, two very different geometries. I selected a manufacturing process and a material for each, predicted the defects each process would produce, and worked out the production volume at which each choice actually pays for itself.

Role

Sole author — analysis, CAD review, documentation

Tools

Autodesk Fusion 360 · MatWeb

Deliverable

10-page technical report

Outcome

CNC + Al 6061-T6 · Injection moulding + optical PC

01 — The brief

Geometry drives process. Volume drives cost.

I was given a set of CAD models and a list of manufacturing processes, and had to pair them up — then defend the pairing. The interesting part isn't the answer, it's that the two parts push in opposite directions: one is a low-volume metal part with a single tolerance that actually matters, and the other is a high-volume plastic part where the tolerance barely matters but the tooling bill decides everything.

I picked the door hinge (CNC machining) and the glasses frames (polymer injection moulding).

02 — Part one

Door hinge → 3-axis CNC machining

The hinge is rectangular wings with two countersunk fastener holes, joined by a cylindrical knuckle with a pin bore. Flat faces, prismatic features, and one bore that has to be round: that is almost a textbook description of a 3-axis milling job. CNC also handles the range of metals a hinge might need, and repeats cleanly across a batch.

Design-for-manufacture analysis — door hinge
Feature Manufacturing concern Predicted defect & effect
Pin bore through the knuckles Tightest tolerance on the part Poor circularity → the pin binds and the hinge doesn't swing freely
Countersunk screw holes Small-diameter tool is fragile Drill breakage → off-centre holes, fastener won't seat flush
Rectangular wings Low stiffness; vibration during face milling Chatter marks, dimensional variation, poor surface finish
Flat wing faces Must stay flat and parallel Warpage → door misalignment at assembly
Fillets at the wing connector End-mill radius must match the design radius Wrong tool radius → undercut, stress concentration at the corner

The decision that mattered

Everything a hinge does depends on one feature: the pin bore. So it can't just be drilled — it has to be drilled and then finish-reamed. Drilling alone leaves a bore that's round enough to look fine and not round enough to swing.

03 — Part two

Glasses frames → polymer injection moulding

The frame is the opposite problem. Thin curved surfaces, a lens rim, a nose bridge, small rounded nose pads, and long slim temple arms — geometry you cannot practically machine, and that plastic flows into beautifully. It's also what the eyewear industry actually does, at volume, for exactly this reason.

Design-for-manufacture analysis — glasses frame
Feature Manufacturing concern Predicted defect & effect
Thin front temple frame Melt struggles to flow evenly through thin sections Short shot; sink marks; warpage along the arm
Thin step at the hinge Thick plastic meets thin plastic — they cool at different rates Sink marks, distortion of the frame
Small rounded nose pads Tight curved detail is hard to fill Incomplete fill; weld lines
Long skinny arms Melt travels a long way down a thin channel Incomplete fill, weld lines, arm warpage
Undercut hinge pockets & lens groove Sharp corners concentrate stress in the steel and the part Accelerated mould wear; frame cracks when flexed at the corner

The decision that mattered

Nearly every defect on that list traces back to one root cause — uneven wall thickness. The fix isn't in the part, it's in the tool: gate into the thickest section at the front of the frame, so the whole cavity fills before the gate freezes off. Hold walls between 1.5–4 mm and most of the table above stops happening.

04 — Material selection

Two materials, chosen against three alternatives each

Process narrows the field; the material has to survive the process and the service life. I benchmarked each choice against the two most plausible alternatives, using published property data rather than intuition.

Door hinge — material trade-off
Criterion Aluminium 6061-T6 ← selected Stainless 304 Carbon steel 1018
Ultimate tensile strength 310 MPa 515 MPa 400 MPa
Density 2.70 g/cc 8.00 g/cc 7.87 g/cc
Corrosion resistance Good (natural oxide) Very good Poor — must be coated
Machinability Excellent — fast, clean chips Medium Good
Material cost $5–15 / kg $15–25 / kg $1–3 / kg
Extra finishing Optional anodising Polishing Paint or galvanising required

6061-T6 loses on raw strength — and it doesn't matter. A domestic door hinge is nowhere near 310 MPa, so the extra strength of steel buys nothing while costing three times the weight. What 6061-T6 wins on is the things that do bear on this part: it machines fast (which is most of the unit cost), it needs no rust coating, and its 276 MPa yield is a wide margin for the actual load. Where it's genuinely weaker — wear resistance — hard anodising closes the gap.

Glasses frame — material trade-off
Criterion Optical polycarbonate ← selected TR-90 nylon ABS
Impact resistance Very high Very high Medium
Clarity 81.6% VLT Mostly opaque Opaque
Heat resistance ~129 °C ~175 °C ~80–100 °C
Density 1.21 g/cc 1.14 g/cc 1.05 g/cc
Resin cost $3–6 / kg $8–15 / kg $2–4 / kg
UV resistance Needs UV package added Good Poor
Best suited to Standard & safety frames Sport / kids' frames Low-cost budget frames

Optical PC wins on the combination the part actually needs: it survives being dropped, it's clear, it flexes at the temple arms without fatiguing, and its 0.63% mould shrinkage is low enough to hold frame geometry. Its one real weakness is moisture — PC absorbs water from the air, and if it isn't dried before moulding that water flashes to steam in the barrel and leaves splay marks on the surface. Dry it at 120 °C, run the mould at 80–100 °C, and the problem disappears.

05 — Cost vs volume

Neither process is cheap. They're cheap at different volumes.

This is the part I found genuinely surprising. For the CNC hinge, almost none of the cost is material — a 200 g billet of 6061-T6 is about a dollar. The cost is setup ($150–200 per run, amortised across the batch) and machine time (~$40–45/hr). For the moulded frame, the resin is pennies and the entire economics live in a $3,300–4,600 mould.

Both curves collapse with volume — but they collapse at completely different scales.

Cost model — underlying figures
Process Volume Cost per unit What dominates the cost
CNC — hinge 1 $160–250 Setup ($150–200) carried by a single part
10–20 $40–80 Machine time (~$40–45/hr, 15–30 min/part)
50–100 $20–35 Machine time; setup now negligible
Injection moulding — frame 100–500 $8–15 Mould tooling ($3,300–4,600) barely amortised
1,000–5,000 $2.60–4.00 Tooling amortising; cycle time
50,000+ $1.00–1.70 Resin + cycle time only ($0.09–0.30 of resin per set)

The conclusion

Injection moulding only makes sense past roughly 500 units. Below that the mould never pays for itself, and for a handful of prototypes 3D printing is both cheaper and faster. The process isn't "better" — it's better at a volume.

06 — What I took away

Three things I got wrong before I got them right

  • A temper designation is not a suffix.

    I had been treating "6061" and "6061-T6" as the same material. They are not: the T6 heat treatment is where most of the strength comes from. If someone writes plain "6061 aluminium" on a drawing and the shop supplies un-tempered stock, the part can come out dramatically weaker than designed — and nothing about the drawing would flag it. The full designation is the spec.

  • Cost lives in setup and cycle time, not in material.

    I assumed the metal was the expensive part of a machined component. It's about a dollar. The other $150–250 is setup and machine hours — which is why the unit cost falls off a cliff between one part and twenty, and why "can we batch this?" is a cost question, not a scheduling one.

  • Geometry decides the process before you get a vote.

    Having modelled both parts, I could see it before I could justify it — you cannot practically machine a thin curved temple arm, and you would never cut a mould for a hinge you need five of. The shape has already narrowed the field. The analysis is how you defend the choice, not how you make it.
